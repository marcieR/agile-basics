Sprint Planning

Sets up the entire team for success. The team decides what can be delivered in the sprint — and how.

👥 Who: Dev team, Scrum Master, Product Owner
📅 When: Beginning of each sprint
⏱️ Duration: ~1 hour per week of sprint
🏗️ Framework: Scrum
Purpose

The product owner brings a prioritized product backlog. The team discusses each item and collectively estimates effort involved. The development team then forecasts how much work can be completed — that body of work becomes the sprint backlog.

💡 Pro Tip

Use sprint planning to flesh out intimate details of the work. Encourage team members to sketch out tasks for all stories, bugs, and tasks. Foster discussions and gather consensus. Effective planning significantly increases the team's chances of success.

☀️

Daily Stand-up

A short, focused daily sync to surface progress and blockers — before they become problems.

👥 Who: Dev team, Scrum Master, Product Owner
📅 When: Daily, typically morning
⏱️ Duration: 15 minutes max
🏗️ Framework: Scrum & Kanban
Three Questions
  • 1What did I complete yesterday?
  • 2What will I work on today?
  • 3Am I blocked by anything?

There's implicit accountability in reporting completed work in front of peers. No one wants to be the person not making progress.

💡 Pro Tip

Some teams use timers. Others toss a ball to keep attention. Many distributed teams use video or group chat to close the distance gap. Your team is unique — your stand-up should be, too!

⚠️

Common Pitfall: The Status Update Trap

When stand-up becomes a reporting exercise rather than a team conversation

⚡ What Goes Wrong

Stand-up drifts into each person reciting what they did yesterday and what they'll do today — directed at the Scrum Master or manager, not the team. It becomes a tick-box exercise. Nobody is actually listening, nobody is connecting the dots, and blockers get glossed over.

The real purpose isn't a status report — it's to understand the work in flight and how it maps to the sprint goal. If no one mentions the sprint goal during stand-up, that's a red flag.

✅ What to do instead
  • 1Anchor to the sprint goal. Start by asking: are we on track to meet our sprint commitment? Everything else should flow from that.
  • 2Talk about the work, not yourself. Walk the board — discuss stories, not individuals. What's in progress? What's stuck? What's at risk?
  • 3Surface blockers immediately. If something is blocked, the team swarms on it — stand-up is where that conversation starts, not where it ends.
  • 4Keep it peer-to-peer. The team talks to each other, not to the Scrum Master. If eyes are on one person, the dynamic is wrong.

Backlog Refinement

The team reviews, clarifies, and estimates upcoming backlog items so they're well-understood and ready before sprint planning begins.

👥 Who: Dev team, Scrum Master, Product Owner
📅 When: Mid-sprint, before next sprint planning
⏱️ Duration: ~1 hour per week of sprint
🏗️ Framework: Scrum
Purpose

Refinement (also called grooming) keeps the product backlog healthy and sprint-ready. The product owner presents upcoming stories, the team asks clarifying questions, and the group estimates complexity — ensuring sprint planning can move fast with well-understood work. Stories that aren't clear enough get sent back for further definition.

  • ?Is this story well enough defined to estimate?
  • ?Do we understand the acceptance criteria?
  • ?Can this be completed within a single sprint?
  • ?Are there dependencies we need to flag?
🎯 Outcome A groomed, prioritised backlog with estimated, clearly-defined stories ready for the next sprint.
💡 Pro Tip

Aim to have 2–3 sprints worth of refined stories in the backlog at all times. This gives the team a buffer and means sprint planning is never blocked waiting on unclear work. The product owner should come prepared — stories without acceptance criteria shouldn't make it into refinement.

⚠️

Common Pitfall: Estimation Conformity

One of the most damaging (and most common) anti-patterns in refinement

⚡ Conformity Bias

One person — often a senior dev, tech lead, or the loudest voice in the room — announces what they think the estimate should be. Everyone else, consciously or not, anchors to that number and follows along.

The result? Estimates that reflect one person's opinion, not the collective wisdom of the team. Hidden complexity goes unspoken. Junior team members with valid concerns stay quiet. The whole point of group estimation is undermined.

✅ How to prevent it
  • 1Use Planning Poker. Everyone reveals their estimate simultaneously — no anchoring, no following the leader.
  • 2Collect estimates privately first. Tools like Jira, Poker Planner, or even hand signals keep estimates hidden until everyone is ready.
  • 3Discuss outliers, not consensus. When estimates differ, dig into why — those gaps reveal the most valuable information.
  • 4Explicitly invite quieter voices. Ask junior team members to share before senior ones. Their perspective often surfaces real complexity.
  • 5Make it safe to disagree. A team culture where challenge is welcomed is the best long-term defence against groupthink.

Story Hydration

The "Three Amigos" review Acceptance Criteria at User Story level to ensure clarity, testability, and readiness before hitting the full team.

👥 Who: PM, Dev Lead, Quality Lead
📅 When: During the sprint
⏱️ Duration: ~30 min per week of sprint
🏗️ Framework: Scrum
Three Amigos Questions
  • ?Is the Acceptance Criteria clear to everyone?
  • ?Can the story be tested / automated?
  • ?Is the User Story ready to be presented to the full team?
🎯 Outcome Understandable, deliverable, and testable Acceptance Criteria at the User Story level.
💡 Why It Matters

Bringing together the PM (business perspective), Dev Lead (technical perspective), and Quality Lead (testing perspective) catches gaps and ambiguities early — before they become expensive rework mid-sprint. The "Three Amigos" approach ensures shared understanding across all angles before the story reaches the broader team.

Decomposition

The full Scrum team breaks User Stories into concrete tasks and subtasks, making them ready for development to begin.

👥 Who: Scrum Team
📅 When: During the sprint
⏱️ Duration: ~30 min per week of sprint
🏗️ Framework: Scrum
Key Questions
  • 1What tasks are involved to complete this User Story?
  • 2Do the tasks align with the Acceptance Criteria?
🎯 Outcome User Stories ready for development to begin — with clear tasks, subtasks, and alignment to Acceptance Criteria.
💡 Why It Matters

Decomposition bridges the gap between a well-defined User Story and actionable development work. Breaking stories into smaller, trackable tasks gives the team clarity on scope, helps surface hidden complexity early, and ensures every task traces back to the agreed Acceptance Criteria — reducing ambiguity at implementation time.

Sprint Review

The team showcases what was built, gathers stakeholder feedback, and celebrates accomplishments.

👥 Who: Dev team, Scrum Master, Product Owner
📅 When: End of each sprint
⏱️ Duration: ~45 min per week of sprint
🏗️ Framework: Scrum & Kanban
Purpose

A sprint review is a time to showcase the work of the team. It can be casual like "demo Fridays" or more structured. This is the time to celebrate accomplishments, demonstrate finished work, and get immediate feedback from stakeholders. Work should be fully demonstrable and meet the team's quality bar to be considered complete.

💡 Pro Tip

Take a casual, celebratory approach. Gather around a team member's desk and watch them demo their new feature. Creating a celebratory atmosphere around demos builds morale and motivates the team to keep shipping great work.

🔍

Sprint Retrospective

Continuous improvement in action — the team reviews what worked, what didn't, and what to do differently.

👥 Who: Dev team, Scrum Master, Product Owner
📅 When: End of each sprint
⏱️ Duration: ~45 min per week of sprint
🏗️ Framework: Scrum & Kanban
Purpose

Agile is about getting rapid feedback to make the product and development culture better. Retrospectives aren't just a time for complaints — they're structured around finding what works so the team can continue to focus there, while finding creative solutions for what doesn't. Continuous improvement is what sustains and drives agile development.

  • ✓What went well this sprint?
  • ↗What could be improved?
  • →What action will we take?
5 Activities
1
Set the Stage

Create a safe environment. Help the team focus and get comfortable sharing openly.

2
Gather Data

Look at what happened — facts, feelings, and events from the sprint without judgement.

3
Generate Insights

Dig into the why. Identify patterns, root causes, and opportunities hidden in the data.

4
Decide What to Do

Pick a small number of concrete actions the team commits to in the next sprint.

5
Close the Retro

Appreciate contributions, summarise actions, and end on a positive note.
