Agile Team Roles

Who Does What
& Why It Matters

Clear role boundaries remove friction. Each person on an agile team has a distinct set of responsibilities — here's what that looks like in practice.

🧭

Scrum Master

The team's coach and guardian of the process. The Scrum Master serves the team — not manages it. Their success is measured by how little the team needs them over time.

Ceremonies 📋 Sprint Planning — Facilitator ☀️ Daily Stand-up — Facilitator 🗂️ Refinement — Facilitator 🎯 Sprint Review — Facilitator 🔍 Retrospective — Facilitator + participant
Core responsibilities
  • 🗓️
    Facilitate all ceremonies Own the scheduling, format, and timebox for all 7 Scrum events. Adjust formats to keep them effective as the team matures.
  • 🚧
    Remove impediments fast Any blocker raised should be actioned within 24 hours. Escalate systemic blockers upward — don't let them fester in the backlog.
  • 🛡️
    Protect the team's focus Shield the team from external interruptions, scope creep, and ad-hoc requests during a sprint. The sprint commitment is sacred.
  • 📚
    Coach agile principles Teach by doing. When the team breaks agile principles, pause and explain the "why" — not just the "what to do instead."
  • 📊
    Track sprint health Monitor velocity, burndown, and team sentiment. Spot trends — not just snapshots — and raise concerns proactively.
Not your job
✕
Assign tasks. The team self-organises and pulls work from the sprint backlog. Task assignment is a command-and-control anti-pattern.
✕
Make product decisions. The PO owns the backlog priority. The SM doesn't decide what gets built or in what order.
✕
Report to management. You serve the team — not translate them for leadership. Progress visibility goes through the PO and sprint reviews.
✕
Own the code or architecture. Technical quality is the team's shared responsibility. The SM doesn't hold veto power over technical decisions.
"

The best Scrum Masters are invisible when everything's working. Their real work shows up in the absence of problems — ceremonies that run themselves, teams that surface blockers early, and retros where people actually say what they think.

— Servant-leader principle
💻

Engineer

Builders of working software. Engineers own the technical quality of the sprint and self-organise to meet commitments. In agile, "engineer" covers all disciplines building the product — engineers, designers, analysts.

Ceremonies 📋 Sprint Planning — Estimator ☀️ Daily Stand-up — Peer update 🗂️ Refinement — Estimator & clarifier 🧩 Decomposition — Lead contributor 🎯 Sprint Review — Demo owner 🔍 Retrospective — Participant
Core responsibilities
  • 🎯
    Self-organise around the sprint goal Pull work from the board — don't wait to be assigned. All decisions about how to meet the sprint commitment are the team's to make.
  • 📏
    Own estimates honestly Estimates belong to the team, not the PO or SM. Never let estimates be negotiated down by pressure. Surface uncertainty explicitly.
  • ✅
    Meet the Definition of Done A story isn't done until it meets every criterion — including tests, code review, and documentation. "Done-ish" ships bugs, not features.
  • 🔊
    Raise blockers immediately Surface blockers at stand-up the day they appear — not at sprint review. The team exists to swarm on blockers. Use it.
  • ⚙️
    Maintain technical excellence Continuous refactoring, green CI, prompt code reviews, and explicit tech-debt tracking in the backlog. Quality is not a separate phase.
Not your job
✕
Commit to impossible timelines. If a deadline is unrealistic, say so in planning — not mid-sprint or at review.
✕
Gold-plate. Build to the acceptance criteria, not to a personal ideal of perfection. Simplicity is an agile principle.
✕
Pick new work before swarming blocked stories. A team ships faster when the board flows — not when individuals are always individually busy.
✕
Accept scope changes mid-sprint without flagging them. Any change to agreed sprint work must surface immediately to the SM and PO.
"

Working software is the primary measure of progress. Not story points completed, not tasks ticked off, not features in staging. Software in users' hands that solves their problem. Everything else is a proxy.

— Agile Principle 7
📋

Product Owner

The single voice of the customer. The PO owns the backlog, defines what gets built and in what order, and is the team's primary interface with business reality. One PO — not a committee.

Ceremonies 📋 Sprint Planning — Backlog presenter ☀️ Daily Stand-up — Optional observer 🗂️ Refinement — Lead presenter 💧 Story Hydration — PM seat (Three Amigos) 🎯 Sprint Review — Host & acceptance lead 🔍 Retrospective — Participant
Core responsibilities
  • 📌
    Own and maintain the backlog Keep 2–3 sprints of refined, clearly-defined, prioritised stories ready at all times. A backlog that's always behind causes planning chaos.
  • 📝
    Write testable acceptance criteria Every story entering refinement must have AC the team can estimate against and QA can test. Vague AC ships ambiguity.
  • 🔢
    Prioritise ruthlessly by value Sequence the backlog by business value, risk, and dependency — not by who asked loudest. Own and explain prioritisation decisions.
  • ⚡
    Be available daily Answer clarifying questions within 4 hours. Unavailable POs cause the team to make assumptions. Wrong assumptions become costly rework.
  • 🎬
    Accept or reject completed work Review completed stories promptly. Acceptance against AC closes the story. Rejection must include specific, actionable feedback.
Not your job
✕
Estimate stories. The team estimates. The PO provides clarity — not effort guesses. PO estimates undermine team ownership.
✕
Change sprint scope mid-sprint. Committed sprint work is off-limits. New priorities go on the backlog for next sprint.
✕
Negotiate estimates down. "Can we do it in 3 points instead of 5?" is not a product conversation. Estimates are the team's honest forecast.
✕
Design or dictate technical solutions. The PO defines the what and why. The team decides the how.
"

Customer collaboration over contract negotiation. The PO is that collaboration made permanent — a full-time representative of what the customer actually needs, constantly prioritising the team's effort toward real value.

— Agile Manifesto, Value 3
🔬

QA / Tester

The team's quality conscience. In agile, quality is not a gate at the end — it's designed in from the start. The QA shifts left: reviewing requirements, writing test cases, and automating before a line of code is written.

Ceremonies 🗂️ Refinement — AC reviewer 💧 Story Hydration — Quality Lead (Three Amigos) 🧩 Decomposition — Test task author ☀️ Daily Stand-up — Peer update 🎯 Sprint Review — Gatekeeper 🔍 Retrospective — Participant
Core responsibilities
  • 🔍
    Review AC before development starts Flag untestable, ambiguous, or incomplete acceptance criteria in hydration — before an engineer picks up the story. This is your earliest and cheapest quality gate.
  • 📄
    Write test cases alongside decomposition Test cases should be ready when development starts — not after it ends. Include edge cases, negative paths, and performance considerations.
  • 🤖
    Automate regression coverage Automation is part of the story — not a separate project. Prioritise automation for high-regression risk paths. Add automation tasks explicitly in decomposition.
  • 🚫
    Gate the sprint review Nothing gets demoed that hasn't passed your testing. Incomplete or failing work doesn't count as done — regardless of how late in the sprint it is.
  • 🤝
    Pair with engineers on edge cases QA isn't an adversary — it's a collaborator. Work alongside engineers during implementation to catch issues before they're committed.
Not your job
✕
Test at the end of the sprint only. Waterfall QA happens last. Agile QA happens throughout. Testing concurrent with development is the standard.
✕
Accept vague acceptance criteria. "The page should feel fast" is not testable. Push for measurable criteria before the story starts.
✕
Automate everything on day one. Prioritise automation strategically — regression-heavy paths first. Exploratory tests don't all need automation.
✕
Let engineers skip tests to meet deadlines. Tests are in the Definition of Done. A story without tests isn't done — it's deferred debt.
"

Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility. Quality isn't a function of testing — it's a consequence of the entire team treating excellence as a shared standard, from the first line of AC to the last line of code.

— Agile Principle 9
👔

Stakeholder

Direction-setters and feedback-givers. Stakeholders provide the "why" — business priorities, funding, and real-world context. Agile works best when stakeholders engage through the right channels and trust the team to execute.

Ceremonies 🎯 Sprint Review — Invited guest & feedback provider 🗂️ Backlog input — Indirect, via PO
Core responsibilities
  • 🎯
    Attend sprint reviews and give specific feedback This is your primary touchpoint. Come prepared with concrete reactions — not vague impressions. "Not what I imagined" starts the right conversation; it doesn't end it.
  • 📬
    Channel all requests through the Product Owner The PO is the single entry point for new work. This protects the team's focus and ensures priorities are weighed consistently.
  • ⚡
    Make decisions promptly when escalated When the PO escalates a decision that needs your input, respond quickly. Delayed decisions become blocked stories.
  • 🏆
    Celebrate shipped work Acknowledging team accomplishments at review builds morale and signals that the work matters. It costs nothing and compounds over time.
  • 🤝
    Trust the team's estimates Velocity and estimates belong to the team. Pressuring for faster output leads to inflated estimates and hidden shortcuts — not actual speed.
Not your job
✕
Talk to engineers directly about new features. Going around the PO fragments priorities and disrupts the team's sprint commitment.
✕
Demand mid-sprint changes. Committed sprint work is off-limits. New priorities go into the backlog and are prioritised by the PO for the next sprint.
✕
Use velocity as a performance metric. Velocity is a capacity planning tool — not a scorecard. Comparing velocity across sprints or teams is misleading.
✕
Skip sprint reviews and ask for written summaries instead. The review is the feedback loop. Absent stakeholders break the inspect-and-adapt cycle.
"

Responding to change over following a plan. Stakeholders who embrace this value become agile accelerators — providing fast, specific feedback and trusting the team to adapt. Those who don't become the biggest source of friction in agile transformations.

— Agile Manifesto, Value 4
