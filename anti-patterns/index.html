What to Avoid

Agile Anti-Patterns

The ceremonies are right. The roles are clear. And yet the team is struggling. These are the patterns that explain why.

1 Scrum But

"We do Scrum, but…" — the team adopts the vocabulary without the discipline. Ceremonies happen on the calendar but not in spirit. The most common form: "we do Scrum but the SM also assigns tasks" or "we do Scrum but we skip retros when we're busy." Scrum works as a system. Removing parts removes the feedback loops that make it work.

🔧
2 Zombie Standup

Standup has become a daily status report directed at the Scrum Master or manager, not a team sync. Each person recites what they did yesterday and what they'll do today — eyes on one person, no one listening. The sprint goal is never mentioned. Blockers get a polite nod and are promptly forgotten. A standup that doesn't surface blockers or mention the sprint goal isn't a standup — it's a ritual.

🧟
3 The Absentee Product Owner

The PO is too busy, too senior, or too disconnected to be available to the team. Questions sit unanswered for days. Engineers make assumptions. Assumptions become features nobody wanted. The single most expensive thing a PO can do is be unavailable — every hour of waiting compounds into rework.

👻
4 Velocity as Performance Metric

Management uses velocity to compare teams, track "productivity," or set delivery commitments. The team responds rationally: estimates inflate, easy stories get prioritised, and velocity rises while actual throughput falls. Velocity is a planning tool for the team — the moment it becomes a KPI for someone else, it stops being useful as either.

📊
5 Mid-Sprint Scope Creep

Work gets added to an active sprint — "it's just a small thing," "the stakeholder needs it this week," "it won't take long." Each exception trains the team that the sprint commitment means nothing. Velocity becomes unpredictable. The team stops committing honestly because they know the sprint will change anyway.

🌊
6 Definition of Done Theater

Stories get marked "Done" without meeting the Definition of Done. Tests aren't written. The PO hasn't reviewed. It's deployed to dev, not staging. The team knows it, the SM knows it, and everyone moves on. The DoD becomes a box-ticking exercise rather than a quality gate. Technical debt accumulates invisibly until it collapses into a production incident.

🎭
7 The HiPPO Effect

The Highest Paid Person's Opinion overrides the team's prioritisation, estimation, and design decisions. The PO can't push back. The team learns not to speak honestly. Backlogs get restructured around executive whims, and the inspect-and-adapt cycle breaks down entirely.

🦛
8 Eternal Refinement

Stories are refined in meeting after meeting but never reach a state where the team feels confident starting them. Analysis paralysis dressed up as process. The root cause is usually that the PO hasn't made a decision — refinement can't substitute for product ownership.

🔄
9 Sprint Carryover Culture

Carrying stories over between sprints becomes the norm rather than the exception. The sprint goal is abandoned. Velocity is consistently overstated in planning. The team loses the habit of committing honestly because they know the sprint boundary is soft. Carryover is a signal — either estimation is poor, scope was changed, or the sprint goal wasn't taken seriously.

📦
10 Siloed QA

Testing happens at the end of the sprint, after engineering is "done." QA is a bottleneck, bugs discovered too late to fix in the sprint, and "done" stories pile up waiting for a test pass that never comes. Agile QA starts at story kick-off, not story completion. If QA isn't in refinement and hydration, they'll always be scrambling.

🚧
11 The Estimation Negotiation

"Can we do it in 3 points instead of 5?" is not a product conversation. When POs or managers negotiate estimates down, the team learns to pad estimates in advance. Honesty disappears from the process. Estimates belong to the team — they are a forecast, not a commitment to be bargained.

🤝
12 The God Sprint

The sprint length keeps growing — from 2 weeks to 3, then 4, then "we'll just do a 6-week sprint for this one." Longer sprints reduce the inspect-and-adapt frequency, hide planning problems, and let bad decisions compound for longer before feedback arrives. Sprint length should match the pace at which requirements change — for most teams, that's 2 weeks.
